Increased student engagement in your class can facilitate improved retention, better critical thinking skills, and improved student learning outcomes. Maintaining student attention on the content requires clear expectations and policies, as well as engaging opportunities for students to interact with the content and their peers. Below are some ideas to help you design an engaging class.

  • In-Class Techniques
  • Experiential Learning

In-Class Techniques

The following strategies can set the stage for an engaging class session:

Classroom Set-Up

Ensure the classroom is set up for interaction and that you can move around to the students or set them up into groups.

  • Best Practices for Classroom Management

Setting Expectations

Ensure that students know your expectations around participating in class and attention in the class. Be sure to go over your expectations on the first day of class.

  • Syllabus Language for Setting Classroom Expectations

Ice-Breakers

Use an ice-breaker on the first day of class to set the tone of participation for the students. Ensure you hear every voice - use the roster to begin to learn names!

  • Ice Breakers

Well-Paced Instruction

Don't rush - students generally need a few moments before they answer and feel very nervous if instruction is rushed. Here are some techniques to slow down instruction.

  • How to Slow Down Instruction

Growth Mindset

Facilitating a growth mindset by encouraging effort and contribution allows student to share more and attempt to answer questions they may be less certain about.

  • Growth Mindset in the Classroom

Addressing Technology

It is crucial to have a technology policy in place for your class. Technology policies, clarify to students the specific rules and expectations in your course related to the use of laptops, computers, phones, or other mobile devices.

N.B. Students with ADS accommodations that include for laptop/tablet use must ALWAYS be permitted to use them regardless of the class policy.

  • Addressing Technology Use in the Classroom

Group Work

Adding Group-Work to class time is an excellent inclusive teaching practice that facilitates better discussions, improved retention, creative problem-solving skills, and increases collaboration and inclusion among students. Try adding it to one class session with the following techniques, your students will thank you!

Experiential Learning

Experiential Learning is a critical addition to all classes hoping to better prepare students for their work in the business world. Experiential learning provides opportunities for students to apply what their learning in your course to real-world problems.

  • Smith Live Cases
  • Guest Speakers
  • Where to go next?

Smith Live Cases

One Experiential learning offering that is unique to Smith is the Smith Live Case. Live Cases are opportunities within the curriculum for students to approach a business partner’s challenging problem and recommend a solution. Each Live Case (primary, extended, or executive) is rigorous, unique, and exciting. In small groups, students work through the constraints and implications of the business situation. They develop recommendations based on the theories or strategies learned in class and present them to the Live Case partner. LiveCases are just one way that OTL’s Experiential Learning team assist faculty with incorporating and designing experiential learning activities into their instruction.

Guest Speakers

OTL can help coordinate guest speakers for your class. Even if you have partnerships in mind, OTL can handle the preparation of the event and provide some preparatory materials for speaker (facing a room of college students can be intimidating!) Guest speakers are a great way to introduce new voices to your instruction and bring concepts to life for your students by hearing professionals discuss the role they’ve played in their professional journey. Similarly, working through the types of problems they face in their profession is helpful to both the students and the speaker!
